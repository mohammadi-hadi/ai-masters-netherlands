# AI & Data Science Master's Programmes in the Netherlands

**Every master's programme in artificial intelligence, machine learning, and data science at Dutch research universities — with how admissions actually work, what it costs, and how to choose.**

The Netherlands has one of the densest AI education landscapes in Europe: a dozen research universities within two hours of each other, nearly everything taught in English, and tuition far below UK/US levels for EU students. It is also confusing from the outside — programme names overlap, half the good options are "specialisations" hiding inside broader degrees, and the deadlines are earlier than most applicants expect.

I did my PhD at Utrecht University and stayed in the Netherlands as a machine learning scientist in industry, and this is the overview I wish had existed when I was comparing options. Every programme below links to its **official page** (aggregator sites are often a year out of date), and every link is checked weekly by CI.

Notes that matter:

- The Open Universiteit option is the only part-time, distance AI master — designed for working professionals, but you need Dutch.

## How Dutch admissions work

**University types.** The Netherlands distinguishes research universities (WO) from universities of applied sciences (HBO). Everything above is WO. If your bachelor is from an HBO institution (or a foreign equivalent of one), most WO masters will require a **pre-master** — typically 30–60 EC of bridging courses — first.

**Degree fit beats grades.** Dutch admissions are mostly about whether your bachelor covered the required material (math, programming, statistics — programme pages list exact expectations), not about beating a GPA cutoff. AI programmes are the strictest; one-year data-science conversions the most flexible.

**Deadlines are earlier than you think.**

- Programmes with a **capped intake (numerus fixus)** close in **mid-January** via Studielink — several AI masters fall in this category in any given year; check the programme page early.
- Non-EU/EEA applicants: typically **January–April**, driven by visa processing.
- EU/EEA applicants: often **April–June**, but scholarship deadlines are months earlier.

**Language.** Everything listed is English-taught except the Open Universiteit AI master. Non-native speakers need IELTS/TOEFL (commonly IELTS 6.5 overall); no Dutch is required to study or, in tech, to work afterwards.

**Applications run through [Studielink](https://www.studielink.nl/)** (the national portal) plus the university's own application system — you usually need both.

## Costs and funding

- **Statutory tuition (EU/EEA students): ~€2,700 per year** (2026–27 statutory rate; set nationally, so it is the same at every university above).
- **Institutional tuition (non-EU/EEA): roughly €15,000–26,000 per year** depending on university and programme — always on the programme's "fees" page.
- **EU students** can access Dutch student finance (DUO) under conditions, including loans and — if you work part-time in NL — more.
- **Scholarships are scarcer than in the US** — there is no widespread TA-ship culture for master's students. The main routes: [Holland Scholarship](https://www.studyinnl.org/) and per-university excellence scholarships (Amsterdam Merit, Utrecht Excellence, Radboud, Maastricht NL-High Potential, etc.) — all very competitive and mostly non-EU-targeted. Apply the moment applications open, not at the deadline.
- **Budget reality**: expect €1,000–1,400/month living costs in the Randstad (Amsterdam, Utrecht, Delft, Rotterdam), less in Enschede, Groningen, Maastricht, Tilburg, Nijmegen.

> **The housing warning nobody puts on their website:** student housing in the Netherlands — especially Amsterdam and Utrecht — is genuinely scarce. Start looking the day you are admitted, use the university's reserved-housing option if one exists (they sell out in minutes), and be extremely suspicious of any landlord who asks for money before a viewing. This, not admissions, is the hardest part of moving here.

## How to choose

The most useful sorting questions, in order:

1. **PhD ambitions?** Take a **two-year** programme. The 120 EC format includes a serious thesis (often embedded in a research group) and usually an internship or research project — exactly what PhD admissions committees and research labs want to see. This is the standard route into Dutch PhD positions (which are salaried jobs here, not studentships).
2. **Career switch from a non-CS field?** The one-year conversions (Utrecht ADS, Tilburg DSS, UvA Information Studies) exist precisely for you. You trade depth for speed; compensate with a strong thesis project and a public portfolio.
3. **Care about a subfield?** The label matters less than the group: NLP is strong in Amsterdam, Groningen and Utrecht; vision and robotics in Delft and Amsterdam; games/agents in Utrecht and Maastricht; statistics-first data science in Leiden. Read the **thesis topics and staff pages** of the group you'd join — that's the honest signal of what a programme is actually good at.
4. **Location.** Everything is close (the country fits in a two-hour train ride), but Amsterdam housing is the hardest and priciest; Enschede, Groningen and Maastricht are the most livable on a student budget.

One thing I'd tell every applicant: in the Dutch system, **your thesis is your CV**. Choose the programme where you can do the most serious final project, not the one with the best-known city name.

## After graduation

- **Non-EU graduates** get access to the **orientation year** (*zoekjaar*): a 12-month residence permit to find work in NL, applied for within three years of graduating. It is one of the most generous post-study schemes in Europe.
- The Dutch ML job market is real and English-speaking: Booking, Adyen, ASML, Philips, ING/ABN, Ahold Delhaize, ElasticSearch, plus a dense startup/scale-up layer and the universities themselves. Salaries are below US levels but so are hours, and contracts are stable.
- PhD positions in the Netherlands are **paid employment** (four-year contracts with salary and benefits) — if research is the goal, a strong Dutch master's thesis is the standard way in.

## FAQ

**Which programme is "the best"?**
Wrong question — there is no meaningful ranking inside this list; all of these are solid, internationally respected degrees. The right question is fit: 1 vs 2 years, the research group behind your likely thesis topic, and where you can afford to live. (Aggregator rankings mostly measure university size and age, not your experience in a 60-person programme.)

**Do I need a CS bachelor for an AI master's?**
For the dedicated AI masters: usually AI, CS, or a clearly quantitative degree with programming — the programme pages list exact requirements, and admissions boards do read transcripts course-by-course. If you don't qualify, the conversion masters and pre-master routes are the designed path, not a lesser one.

**Do I need to speak Dutch?**
No — not for any English-taught programme, not for daily life, and mostly not for tech jobs afterwards. Learning some anyway pays off socially and eventually professionally.

**Is a one-year master's taken seriously by employers?**
In the Netherlands, yes — employers here understand the WO system and read the thesis/projects, not the duration. If you might later apply for PhDs **abroad** (e.g., US), the two-year research format travels better.

**Can I work while studying?**
EU students: freely. Non-EU students: capped part-time hours with a work permit arranged by the employer (or full-time only in summer) — factor this into budgets rather than counting on it.
